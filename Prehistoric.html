Giant Prehistoric Fish

Leedsichthys

Leedsichthys was a prehistoric fish that lived during the Late Jurassic period, approximately 165 million years ago. It is considered one of the largest bony fish to have ever existed, with estimates suggesting it could grow up to 16.8m long. Leedsichthys had gill rakers—specialized structures in its mouth—to strain massive quantities of plankton from seawater, similar to modern filter-feeding fish like whale sharks and basking sharks.

Fun Fact: Because its skeleton was mostly made of cartilage, like modern sharks and rays, fossils of Leedsichthys are rare and often incomplete. This has made studying this ancient fish a puzzle for paleontologists, with much of its size and lifestyle reconstructed from a few key specimens!

Megalodon

Megalodon was a prehistoric shark and one of the largest and most fearsome predators to ever swim the oceans. It lived approximately 23 to 3.6 million years ago during the Miocene and Pliocene epochs. Estimates suggest megalodon could reach lengths of up to 18 meters, dwarfing even the largest modern great white sharks. Its massive jaws, filled with serrated teeth up to 18 centimeters long, were powerful enough to crush the bones of whales, its primary prey. Megalodon was a global predator, thriving in warm, shallow seas and preying on a wide range of marine life. Fossilized teeth are its most common remains, often found on beaches and in sediment layers.

Fun Fact: The bite force of megalodon is estimated to have been over 40,000 pounds per square inch (psi), making it one of the strongest bites in the history of life on Earth. For comparison, this is over ten times the bite force of a modern great white shark!

Mosasaurus

Mosasaurus was a massive marine reptile that lived approximately 82 to 66 million years ago . Reaching lengths of up to 17 meters, it was one of the largest predators of its time, dominating the ancient oceans. Unlike fish or sharks, Mosasaurus was a reptile closely related to modern monitor lizards and snakes. This apex predator had a streamlined, elongated body, flippers for steering, and a powerful tail fin for propulsion. Mosasaurus preyed on a variety of marine creatures, including fish, ammonites, other marine reptiles, and even birds.

Fun Fact: Mosasaurus had a double-hinged jaw and flexible skull, similar to modern snakes, allowing it to swallow prey larger than its head! This incredible adaptation made it a highly effective hunter in the prehistoric oceans.

Catfish

Ichthyosaurus, meaning "fish lizard," was a marine reptile that lived approximately 200 to 190 million years ago during the Early Jurassic period. Despite its name, Ichthyosaurus was not a fish but a reptile, adapted to life in the oceans. Growing up to 3 meters long, it had a streamlined, dolphin-like body with a long snout filled with sharp teeth, perfect for catching fish and squid. Fossils of Ichthyosaurus have been found in Europe, providing valuable insights into its lifestyle and evolutionary history.

Fun Fact: Ichthyosaurus had some of the largest eyes relative to its body size of any animal, giving it exceptional vision. This adaptation helped it hunt in deep or dimly lit waters, making it a formidable predator in its marine environment.
